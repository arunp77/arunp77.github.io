Introduction

Oceanography, the study of the physical, chemical, biological, and geological aspects of the ocean, plays a crucial role in understanding Earth's complex marine environment. This field leverages a variety of methodologies and technologies, including remote sensing, to collect, analyze, and interpret oceanic data.

Fundamentals of Oceanography

  1. Physical Oceanography: Physical oceanography deals with the study of ocean currents, waves, and tides. It explores the interactions between the ocean and the atmosphere, which significantly influence climate patterns.
  2. Chemical Oceanography: Chemical oceanography focuses on the chemical composition of seawater and the biogeochemical cycles. It examines the sources and sinks of various chemical elements and compounds in the ocean.
  3. Biological Oceanography: Biological oceanography investigates the marine organisms and their interactions with the ocean environment. It covers the study of marine ecosystems, food webs, and the impact of human activities on marine life.
  4. Geological Oceanography: Geological oceanography examines the structure and composition of the ocean floor. It includes the study of plate tectonics, underwater volcanism, and sediment processes.

Remote Sensing in Oceanography

Remote sensing is a powerful tool in oceanography, providing comprehensive data over large spatial and temporal scales. It involves collecting data from satellites, aircraft, and autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs).

Types of Remote Sensing

  1. Satellite Remote Sensing: Satellites equipped with sensors collect data on sea surface temperature, sea level, ocean color, and ice cover. Examples include NASA's Aqua and Terra satellites, and ESA's Sentinel satellites.
  2. Aircraft Remote Sensing: Aircraft Remote Sensing: Aircraft can be equipped with sensors to collect high-resolution data on smaller spatial scales. They are used for targeted studies and can provide detailed imagery of coastal and shelf areas.
  3. Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs): AUVs collect data on the subsurface environment, including temperature, salinity, and ocean currents. They provide detailed vertical profiles and can operate in areas inaccessible to ships.

Key Remote Sensing Instruments

  • Radiometers: Measure the intensity of radiation, providing data on sea surface temperature and ocean color.
  • Radar Altimeters: Measure sea surface height, which is crucial for understanding ocean circulation and sea level rise.
  • LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging): Uses laser pulses to measure the depth and topography of the ocean floor.

Data Sources in Oceanography

Oceanographic data come in various formats, commonly including: NetCDF (Network Common Data Form), HDF (Hierarchical Data Format), GRIB (GRIdded Binary). Oceanographic data are collected from a variety of sources, including:
  1. In-Situ Measurements
    • Buoys and Drifters: Measure parameters like temperature, salinity, and current velocity.
    • Research Vessels: Conduct detailed surveys and collect samples from different ocean depths.
  2. Satellite Data
    • MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer): Provides data on ocean color, sea surface temperature, and chlorophyll concentration.
    • TOPEX/Poseidon and Jason-1, 2, 3: Measure sea surface height and provide insights into ocean circulation.
  3. Global Observing Systems
    • ARGO: A global array of profiling floats that measure temperature and salinity in the upper 2000 meters of the ocean.
    • GOOS (Global Ocean Observing System): Provides a framework for coordinating ocean observations worldwide.

Oceanography is an interdisciplinary field that utilizes advanced technologies, including remote sensing, to study and understand the ocean. By integrating data from various sources and using sophisticated data formats, oceanographers can monitor, analyze, and predict changes in the marine environment. This knowledge is crucial for managing marine resources, mitigating the impacts of climate change, and protecting ocean health.

Sentinel 3 : Satellite

Sentinel-3 is an European Earth Observation satellite mission developed to support Copernicus ocean, land, atmospheric, emergency, security and cryospheric applications. It is jointly operated by ESA and EUMETSAT to deliver operational ocean and land observation services. There are two satellites lanuched: Sentinel-3A on 16 February 2016 Sentinel-3B on 25 April 2018.
  • Main objectives: Systematically measures Earth's oceans, land, ice, and atmosphere. with
    • Measure sea surface topography
    • Measure sea and land surface temperature.
    • Measure ocean and land surface color.
  • Ensures high accuracy and reliability.
  • Supports ocean forecasting systems, environmental monitoring, and climate monitoring.
  • Additional applications: Sea-level change & sea-surface temperature mapping, water quality management, sea-ice extent and thickness mapping and numerical ocean prediction; land-cover mapping, vegetation health monitoring; glacier monitoring; water resource monitoring; wildfire detection; numerical weather prediction.

Sentinel-3 Instruments:

Four instruments working in synergy.
  • OLCI: Ocean and Land Colour Instrument
  • SLSTR: Sea and Land Surface Temperature Instrument
  • SRAL: SAR Radar ALtimeter
  • MWR: Microwave Radiometer
These are complemented by three instruments for Precise Orbit Determination (POD):
  • DORIS: a Doppler Orbit Radio positioning system
  • A GNSS receiver, providing precise orbit determination and tracking multiple satellites simultaneously
  • A Laser Retro-Reflector (LRR) system to accurately locate the satellite in orbit using
  1. Sea and Land Surface Temperature Radiometer (SLSTR):
    • Measures global sea- and land-surface temperatures daily with an accuracy better than 0.3 K.
    • Continues Envisat’s Advanced Along Track Scanning Radiometer legacy.
    • Includes two thermal infrared channels for active fire detection and fire radiative power measurement.
    • Supports Copernicus Emergency Response and Climate Services.
    • covering 9 spectral bands (550–12 000 nm), dual-view scan with swath widths of 1420 km (nadir) and 750 km (backwards), and a spatial resolution of 500 m for visible and near-infrared, and 1 km for thermal infrared channels
  2. Ocean and Land Colour Instrument (OLCI):
    • Based on Envisat’s Medium Resolution Imaging Spectrometer heritage.
    • Features 21 distinct bands in the 0.4–1.02 μm spectral region (400 - 1020 nm).
    • Spatial resolution of 300 m for all measurements.
    • Swath width of 1270 km, overlapping with SLSTR swath.
    • covering 21 spectral bands (400–1020 nm) with a swath width of 1270 km and a spatial resolution of 300 m.
    • Monitors ocean ecosystems, supports crop management and agriculture, and provides estimates of atmospheric aerosol and clouds.
    • Delivers significant societal benefits through informed decision-making.
  3. Dual-Channel Microwave Radiometer (MWR):
    • dual frequency at 23.8 & 36.5 GHz
    • Supports the SAR altimeter.
    • Derives atmospheric correction and atmospheric column water vapour measurements.
    • Ensures the demanding performance requirements for Sentinel-3 are met.
    • Helps achieve accurate measurements of sea surface height and other parameters.
  4. Synthetic Aperture Radar Altimeter (SAR):
    • Measures the height of the sea surface, waves, and surface wind speed over oceans.
    • Provides accurate topography measurements over sea ice, ice sheets, rivers, and lakes.
    • Utilizes dual-frequency (Ku and C band) technology.
    • Ku-band (300 m after SAR processing) and C-band with a spatial resolution of approx 300 m
    • Based on heritage from the CryoSat and Jason missions.
    • Delivers measurements at approximately 300 m resolution in the along-track direction after SAR processing.
    • First satellite altimeter to provide 100% coverage over all of Earth’s surfaces in SAR mode.
